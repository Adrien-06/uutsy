the best cat scratchers in 2026.

june 2026 · 6 min read

the market for cat scratchers has expanded considerably over the past few years, but the quality gap has expanded with it. there are more options than ever — and more ways to make the wrong choice. this guide breaks down the main scratcher types honestly, explains what cats actually respond to, and makes the case for why not all scratchers belong in the same category.

the goal is not to rank products by star ratings. it is to give you a clear framework for evaluating what you bring into your home — for your cat's wellbeing, and for your own.

vertical posts: the standard — and its limits

the vertical scratching post is the most common cat scratcher format, and for good reason. cats naturally want to reach up and drag their claws downward in a long, full-body stretch. a tall post accommodates that instinct when built to the right height — roughly the length of your cat's body when fully extended, which for most cats means at least 60 to 70 centimetres.

the problem is stability. most budget vertical posts are too narrow at the base, which means they tip when a cat leans into them with force. one bad experience is often enough to put a cat off a scratcher entirely. sisal rope posts wear out unevenly and can unravel in ways that catch claws. carpet-covered posts carry the unintended consequence of teaching cats that carpet is a valid scratching surface.

a good vertical post is tall, firmly weighted, and covered in either natural sisal or high-density corrugated cardboard. those that double as a perch add value — cats will naturally return to a post that offers both a scratching surface and a resting spot at height.

horizontal pads: underrated but incomplete

flat cardboard scratch pads placed on the floor are often dismissed as cheap solutions, but they serve a genuinely different function. many cats prefer to scratch horizontally, particularly first thing in the morning when they stretch low and pull their weight forward. a horizontal pad placed near a sleeping area will often be used consistently by cats who ignore vertical posts entirely.

the drawback is durability and design. standard corrugated cardboard pads shed fine dust as they break down, which accumulates quickly and requires frequent replacement. they also offer no resting function, and their flat profile means they can slide across hard floors during use unless backed with rubber or placed against a wall.

high-density versions perform significantly better — the compressed corrugation holds up longer, produces less debris, and gives more satisfying resistance under claw. still, a horizontal pad alone rarely meets all of a cat's scratching and resting needs.

lounge and sofa formats: a different category entirely

the scratcher lounge — a low-profile sofa-shaped piece that combines a scratching surface with a resting platform — represents a different design philosophy. it is not a compromise between a scratcher and a bed. it is a recognition that cats move fluidly between activity and rest, and that furniture should accommodate both without asking the cat to relocate.

a well-designed scratcher lounge gives a cat a place to stretch and scratch along the sides, a platform to lie on, and a backrest to lean against. the low footprint means it integrates into living spaces rather than dominating them. and because the cat uses it frequently for rest, the scratching surface gets consistent use without any training required.

what cats actually prefer — and why it matters

studies in feline behaviour consistently show that cats prefer scratching surfaces that offer resistance, allow full extension, and are located in socially prominent areas of the home. they scratch to communicate — to leave visual marks and scent signals in places that matter to them. a scratcher placed in an isolated back room fails on every count.

texture preference varies by individual. some cats are drawn immediately to sisal; others are indifferent to it and respond strongly to corrugated cardboard. if your cat has a favourite surface to scratch illegally — the arm of a sofa, the base of a door frame — note the texture and replicate it. rough-weave fabric and tight corrugation are the two most common preferences.
