cat furniture that actually fits your home.

june 2026 · 5 min read

if you have spent any time searching for cat furniture, you will have noticed a pattern. cat beds come in shades of plush lilac and synthetic fleece. cat houses are miniature alpine lodges or corrugated plastic cubes. cat wall shelves are particleboard brackets with carpet offcuts stapled on. the options are functional, sometimes — but rarely beautiful, and almost never something you would choose to display in the rooms you care about.

this is not a superficial concern. where you place cat furniture shapes whether your cat uses it. a cat bed pushed into a corner of the utility room is a cat bed that gets ignored. the same piece, placed near the window where afternoon light pools, becomes a favourite. design and placement are inseparable.

the problem with most cat beds

most cat beds are designed for the pet aisle, not for the home. the brief is usually softness and price point. the result is something machine-washable and structurally compromised — a circular cushion that deflates within a month, or a hooded dome that collapses the first time a heavier cat leans against it.

what cats actually want from a resting surface is support and a sense of enclosure. they like to feel held, not simply placed. a bed with structure — firm sides, a raised back, a base that doesn't compress — gives a cat the security it seeks. and that kind of structural quality is far more achievable in natural materials than in synthetic ones.

what litter box furniture gets right (and wrong)

litter box furniture has taken a more considered approach to the design problem. pieces made to conceal a litter tray are often built from real wood or mdf with clean finishes, because they need to pass as side tables or storage units in a real room. the best examples are genuinely indistinguishable from general furniture at a glance.

the same logic should extend to every piece of cat furniture, not just the ones that need to hide something. a cat house does not need to look like a doll's cottage. a cat bed does not need to announce itself with pastel tones. the vocabulary of good interior design — neutral palette, honest materials, clean form — applies here exactly as it does elsewhere.

cat wall shelves and vertical space

cat wall shelves are among the more elegant solutions available to cat owners with considered interiors. mounted at the right heights, they allow cats to climb, survey the room, and rest at elevation — all behaviours they engage in naturally. the best designs use solid wood or metal brackets with simple platforms, sized to support the full body of the cat.

the challenge with wall shelves is installation and commitment. they require wall anchoring, and removing them leaves marks. for renters, or for cat owners who like to rearrange a room, a floor-based piece offers more flexibility without sacrificing the same design quality.

the case for a single considered piece

rather than accumulating a cat bed here, a scratcher there, and a play structure in the corner, there is a strong argument for investing in one well-designed piece that serves multiple functions. a scratcher that is also a lounge, built in natural materials with furniture-grade construction, can live in the living room without apology.
